About the Project

Project Overview

This project explores patterns in reported UFO sightings across the United States, focusing on how sightings vary over time and location. Specifically, we investigate how the time of day influences the frequency of sightings and which regions of the country experience higher concentrations of reports.

UFO sightings are a compelling subject because they exist at the intersection of science, perception, and culture. While some interpret these events as potential evidence of extraterrestrial activity, others attribute them to atmospheric phenomena, aircraft, or cognitive bias. By analyzing large-scale reporting data, this project aims to uncover meaningful patterns that help explain when and where sightings are most likely to occur.

Dataset

About the Data

The dataset used in this project comes from the National UFO Reporting Center (NUFORC) and contains approximately 80,000 reported UFO sightings spanning over a century. Each record includes a rich set of attributes that allow analysis across time, location, and descriptive characteristics.

Results

Key Findings & Future Work

⏱ Temporal Patterns

Sightings are more concentrated in summer months, indicating a strong seasonal trend. Variation across individual weeks within a month is minimal once differences in week length are accounted for.

🌙 Time of Day

Sightings are significantly more frequent during evening and late-night hours, suggesting that visibility conditions and human behavior—such as being outdoors or looking at the sky—play an important role.

📍 Geographic Clustering

Sightings are not evenly distributed across the United States. They tend to cluster in more populated regions and areas with higher observational opportunities.

🔤 Descriptive Patterns

Reports often rely on a small set of recurring terms such as lights, circles, and triangles, indicating that people may interpret and describe unfamiliar aerial phenomena in similar ways.

🔭 Future Work

This analysis could be expanded by incorporating additional contextual data, such as weather conditions, population density, or air traffic patterns. Further work could also explore sentiment or linguistic patterns in sighting descriptions to gain deeper insight into how people perceive and report these events.
