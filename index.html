Mapping the Unknown: Temporal and Regional Trends in UFO Reports

Project Overview

This project explores patterns in reported UFO sightings across the United States, focusing on how sightings vary over time and location. Specifically, we investigate how the time of day influences the frequency of sightings and which regions of the country experience higher concentrations of reports. UFO sightings are a compelling subject because they exist at the intersection of science, perception, and culture. While some interpret these events as potential evidence of extraterrestrial activity, others attribute them to atmospheric phenomena, aircraft, or cognitive bias. By analyzing large-scale reporting data, this project aims to uncover meaningful patterns that help explain when and where sightings are most likely to occur.

About the Data

The dataset used in this project comes from the National UFO Reporting Center (NUFORC) and contains approximately 70,000 reported UFO sightings spanning over a century. Each record includes attributes such as datetime, city, state, country, shape, duration, comments, and geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude). This rich set of attributes allows us to analyze sightings across multiple dimensions, including time, location, and descriptive characteristics of each report.

Key Findings & Future Work

Through our analysis, we found that UFO sightings exhibit clear patterns across both time and geography. Temporally, sightings are more concentrated in the summer months, indicating a strong seasonal trend, while variation across individual weeks within a month is relatively minimal once differences in week length are accounted for. Additionally, sightings are significantly more frequent during evening and late-night hours, suggesting that visibility conditions and human behavior—such as being outdoors or looking at the sky— play an important role in when sightings are reported.

From a geographic perspective, sightings are not evenly distributed across the United States. Instead, they tend to cluster in more populated regions and areas with higher observational opportunities. The shape and description analyses further show that reports often rely on a small set of recurring terms, such as lights, circles, and triangles, indicating that people may interpret and describe unfamiliar aerial phenomena in similar ways.

In the future, this analysis could be expanded by incorporating additional contextual data, such as weather conditions, population density, or air traffic patterns, to better understand the factors that influence where and when sightings occur. Further work could also explore sentiment or linguistic patterns in the sighting descriptions to gain deeper insight into how people perceive and report these events.
