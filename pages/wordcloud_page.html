UFO Word Cloud: Descriptions of Sightings

Data source
This visualization uses scrubbed.csv from the National UFO Reporting Center (NUFORC), containing approximately 70,000 UFO sighting reports with attributes such as datetime, location, and comments.
How the data was prepared
The word cloud is generated from the comments field. Data was filtered to U.S. sightings from 2010 onward with durations longer than two minutes. Text was cleaned by removing noise such as URLs and combining all descriptions into a single corpus for analysis.
Interpretation
Larger words indicate more frequently used terms. The rankings below break out color and shape language to show how witnesses describe what they saw.

Why color words matter Orange and red dominate, likely reflecting the warm glow of distant lights at night, consistent with aircraft, flares, or atmospheric effects. White is also very common, appearing when witnesses describe high-altitude or bright point sources. Cool colors like blue and green are notably less frequent, suggesting they are rarer or less salient in descriptions. Color patterns can help researchers distinguish between categories of sightings and cross-reference with known aerial phenomena.

Why shape words matter "Light" overwhelmingly dominates shape descriptions, suggesting most sightings are of ambiguous point sources rather than structured craft. Triangle and fireball are the next most common, triangle likely reflects a culturally iconic UFO shape, while fireball may overlap with meteor or re-entry sightings. Rarer geometric terms like disk, oval, and cigar appear less frequently but are historically associated with classic UFO archetypes. Comparing shape and color distributions together can reveal whether certain shapes are associated with particular colors.

Takeaway

This visualization highlights the most common words used in UFO sighting descriptions, revealing consistent patterns in how people interpret and report unexplained events. Terms such as "light," "object," "orange," and "flying" dominate the dataset, suggesting that sightings are often described in terms of brightness, motion, and color rather than clearly identifiable structures. The color ranking shows that warm tones — orange (3,521), red (2,423), and white (1,446) — far outnumber cool tones, while the shape ranking confirms that vague descriptors like "light" (3,054) vastly outnumber specific geometric forms. Together, these patterns suggest people rely on a shared and limited vocabulary to describe ambiguous aerial phenomena.
